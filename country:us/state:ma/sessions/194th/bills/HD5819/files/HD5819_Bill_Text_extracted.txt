Report to the Joint Committee on Election Laws
On Jail-Based Voting in the
2024 State Primary & State Election
Office of Secretary of the Commonwealth
One Ashburton Place, Room 1705, Boston, Massachusetts 02108 • (617) 727-2828

Purpose
Pursuant to Section 25C(g) of Chapter 54 of the General Laws, the Elections Division of
the Office of the Secretary of the Commonwealth submits the following report on voting
in jails, prisons, and other correctional facilities in Massachusetts in the 2024 State
Primary and State Election.
As required by law, this report includes anonymized, aggregated data on the number of:
(i) eligible incarcerated voters at the time of the election in each municipality; and (ii)
incarcerated voters who requested an early voting by mail or absent voting ballot and the
outcome of that request in each municipality, including the reasons for rejection, if
applicable.
Methodology
To identify incarcerated voters who applied for an early or absentee ballot, lists of
eligible voters were matched against absentee and early voter lists for each election.
While incarcerated voters were provided with absentee ballot applications at their
facilities, a voter or a family member of a voter may request an absentee ballot using any
form of written communication. For those voters who are registered to vote, an early
Vote by Mail ballot may be requested in the same manner. As such, it was necessary to
search both the absentee and early voter lists, regardless of the mailing address of the
ballot.
Voters were matched by name and date of birth (where available). Similar name matches
were included and manually reviewed, in order to identify voters who may be listed with
slightly different names in each list.
Identification numbers did not prove to be useful in matching voters, as facilities collect
and report the last four digits of each person’s social security number. Due to the
requirements of the Help America Vote Act of 2002 and the impact of automatic voter
registration through the Registry of Motor Vehicles, the vast majority of voter records in
Massachusetts contain a driver’s license number instead of a social security number.
2

Residency & Eligibility
Under Section 1 of Chapter 51 of the General Laws, a Massachusetts resident who is
otherwise eligible to register to vote, but is currently confined to a correctional facility for
a reason other than a felony conviction may be considered a “specially qualified voter.”
Specially qualified voters are eligible absentee voters who are exempt from voter
registration requirements. Thus, an incarcerated voter who is not currently registered to
vote may vote as an unregistered absentee voter.
An incarcerated voter’s residence for voting purpose is presumed to be the last place that
the voter resided prior to their incarceration, rather than at the facility in which they are
detained. An incarcerated voter’s residence for voting purpose is presumed to be the last
place that the voter resided prior to their incarceration, rather than at the facility in which
they are detained.1
Eligible Voters
As required by Section 25C(f) of Chapter 54, the Elections Division compiled data from
each facility in Massachusetts. The Department of Corrections and each county sheriff,
with the assistance of the Massachusetts Sheriffs’ Association, provided for person
incarcerated by reason of a misdemeanor conviction or detained pre-trial in each facility,
as of the close of voter registration for the 2024 State Primary and 2024 State Election,
the following information:
• Name
• Date of Birth
• Last 4 digits of social security number of driver’s license number, if any
• Address on file
In order to determine eligibility for voting, the Elections Division also requested and
received information on the citizenship status of each incarcerated person.

Section 25C(f) further requires each facility to provide information on those who were
incarcerated due to a felony conviction who were either newly incarcerated or discharged
since the previous report. As those incarcerated by reason of a felony conviction are not
eligible to vote, and as those who have been discharged from the facility are no longer
incarcerated, information regarding those voters is not included in this report.
After compiling all lists of potentially eligible voters and excluding those whose
residence was outside the Commonwealth, those who were not citizens of the United
States, and those who were not yet 18 years of age by the relevant election date, it was
determined that there were 5,590 eligible voters2 incarcerated at the time of the
September 3, 2024 State Primary and 4,647 eligible voters3 incarcerated at the time of
the November 5, 2024 State Election.
Tables showing the number of eligible incarcerated voters by municipality are included in

Ballot Statistics – State Primary
After matching the list of eligible voters against the absentee and early voter lists, it was
determined that 159 incarcerated individuals applied for a 2024 State Primary ballot.
Of the 159 ballots that were mailed to incarcerated voters, 58 were returned to local
election officials and counted, while 86 were not returned, and 15 were rejected.
Of the 15 rejected ballots, 9 were rejected due to a failed delivery, indicating that the
ballot was returned to the local election office by the postal service. Of the 6 ballots that
were returned to local election offices by voters and rejected, 3 were rejected because
they were received after the close of polls on September 3, 2024, 2 were rejected because

Ballot Statistics – State Election
After matching the list of eligible voters against the absentee and early voter lists, it was
determined that 422 incarcerated individuals applied for a 2024 State Election ballot.
Of the 422 ballots that were mailed to incarcerated voters, 230 were returned to local
election officials and counted, while 165 were not returned, and 27 were rejected.
Of the 27 rejected ballots, 10 were rejected due to a failed delivery, indicating that the
ballot was returned to the local election office by the postal service. Of the 17 ballots that
were returned to local election offices by voters and rejected, 6 were rejected because
they were received too late, 6 were rejected because the voter had not signed the ballot
envelope, 3 were rejected because the ballot was returned without a ballot envelope, and
2 were rejected for some other reason. While local election officials do not report the
specific reason when selecting “Other” as the reason for rejection, one voter’s record
indicated that he had been convicted of a felony after the ballot was mailed.
Of the 230 ballots that were returned and accepted, 148 ballots were mailed to the voter
at the facility, while 76 were mailed to the voter’s home address, and 6 were mailed to a
different mailing address.

Of the 165 ballots that went unreturned, 109 were mailed to the voter at the facility, while
55 were mailed to the voter’s home address, and 1 was mailed to a different mailing
address.
Tables containing these statistics by municipality are included in Appendix D and
Appendix F.
Other Findings
In reviewing the lists submitted by correctional facilities, it was discovered that some
incarcerated voters are registered to vote at the facility at which they are incarcerated. As
discussed above, incarcerated voters generally should not be registering or voting from
the address of the facility.
Of the 159 voters who applied for State Primary ballots, 40 were registered or voting
from the address of their facility. The largest numbers were in Suffolk County, with 22
such voters, and the Department of Corrections, with 13 such voters.
Of the 422 voters who applied for State Election ballots, 83 were registered at or voting
from the address of their facility. The largest numbers were again in Suffolk County, with
52 such voters, and the Department of Corrections, with 22 such voters.
It is understood that these voters may have been provided with incorrect information
about their residence for voting purposes, leading them and others to register to vote at
the facility. The Elections Division intends to communicate with the facilities to ensure
that voters are provided with the proper guidance, by staff and by other groups assisting
with voting, in order to ensure that incarcerated voters are not casting ballots in precincts
and districts where they are not eligible to vote.
It was also noted that the higher than expected number of ballots mailed to voters’ home
addresses and unreturned may have been caused by the mailing of Vote by Mail
applications to voters at the beginning of 2024, ahead of the 2024 Presidential Primaries.
It is likely that some number of voters requested ballots for all 2024 elections to be
mailed to them at their home address prior to being incarcerated.
