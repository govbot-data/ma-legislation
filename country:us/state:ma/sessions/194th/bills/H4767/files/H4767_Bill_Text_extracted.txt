An Act requiring health care employers to develop and implement programs to prevent
workplace violence.
Whereas, The deferred operation of this act would tend to defeat its purpose, which is to
require health care employers to develop and implement programs to prevent workplace
violence, therefore it is hereby declared to be an emergency law, necessary for the immediate
preservation of the public safety.
Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives in General Court assembled, and by the authority
of the same, as follows:

32 “Serious bodily injury”, bodily injury which results in a permanent disfigurement,
33 protracted loss or impairment of a bodily function, limb or organ or substantial risk of death.
34 “Workplace violence”, conduct at the work site that: (i) is an unpermitted or harmful
35 touching of another person; (ii) is an attempt or act to use some degree of physical force on
36 another person; or (iii) could be reasonably perceived as an intent to touch without permission,
37 use immediate physical force or injure a particular person now or in the future, that if carried out
38 would constitute a crime, and that causes another person to reasonably believe that the person
39 has the intent and ability to carry out such conduct.
40 (b) Annually, each health care employer shall perform a facility-specific risk assessment
41 that shall include, but shall not be limited to, the standards determined by the department, in
42 consultation with the office of health equity. The facility-specific risk assessment shall be
43 performed in cooperation with the employees of the health care employer and any labor
44 organization representing the employees, examining all factors that may put any of the
45 employees at risk of workplace violence. The factors shall include, but shall not be limited to: (i)
46 working in public settings; (ii) guarding or maintaining property or possessions; (iii) working in
47 high-crime areas; (iv) working late night or early morning hours; (v) working alone or in small
48 numbers; (vi) uncontrolled public access to the workplace; (vii) working in public areas where
49 people are in crisis; (viii) working in areas where a patient or resident may exhibit violent
50 behavior; (ix) working in areas with known security problems; and (x) working with insufficient
51 qualified staff in 1 or more position titles to address foreseeable risk factors.
52 (c) Based on the findings of the risk assessment in subsection (b), the health care
53 employer shall develop and implement a program to minimize the danger of workplace violence
3 of 14

54 to employees, which shall include, but shall not be limited to, appropriate employee training and
55 a system for the ongoing reporting and monitoring of incidents and situations involving violence
56 or the risk of violence. Employee training shall include, but shall not be limited to, in addition to
57 all employer training program policies, methods of reporting to appropriate public safety
58 officials, bodies or agencies and processes necessary for the filing of criminal charges.
59 (d) Each health care employer shall develop a written plan setting forth the employer’s
60 workplace violence prevention plan. The health care employer shall make the plan available to
61 each employee and provide the plan to any of its employees upon written request. The health
62 care employer shall provide, upon written request, the plan to any labor organization or
63 organizations representing any of its employees. The plan shall include, but shall not be limited
64 to: (i) a list of those factors and circumstances that may pose a danger to employees; (ii) a
65 description of the methods that the health care employer will use to alleviate hazards associated
66 with each factor, including, but not limited to, employee training and any appropriate changes in
67 job design, staffing, security, equipment or facilities; (iii) a post-incident debriefing process with
68 affected staff; and (iv) a description of the reporting and monitoring system.
69 (e) Each health care employer shall designate a senior manager responsible for the
70 development and support of an in-house crisis response team for employee victims of workplace
71 violence. The in-house crisis response team shall implement an assaulted staff action program
72 that shall include, but shall not be limited to, group crisis interventions, individual crisis
73 counseling, staff victims’ support groups, employee victims’ family crisis intervention, peer-help
74 and professional referrals.

124 (b) A health care employer shall permit an employee to take paid leave from work if the
125 employee: (i) is a victim of assault or assault and battery and such assault or assault and battery

130 (c)(1) An employee seeking leave under this section shall not be required to use annual
131 leave, vacation leave, personal leave or sick leave available to the employee, prior to requesting

133 entitlement to eligible paid family and medical leave under chapter 175M.
134 (2) No health care employer shall require an employee to exhaust all annual leave,
135 vacation leave, personal leave or sick leave available to the employee prior to requesting or
136 taking leave under this section; provided, that said paid leave shall be concurrent with paid
137 family and medical leave under chapter 175M.
138 (d) A health care employer may require an employee to provide documentation

141 employee shall provide such documentation to the health care employer within 5 business days

143 (e) An employee seeking leave from work pursuant to subsection (b) shall provide
