Dear Chairs Crighton, Arciero, Rodrigues, and Michlewitz,
The Board of Directors of the Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority (MBTA) in March 2024
approved a fare change expanding reduced fares to income-eligible riders on all modes and
applying reduced fares to The RIDE paratransit service for Seniors and income-eligible riders. This
expansion of reduced fares launched for MBTA riders in early-September 2024. This program is
commonly referred to as “Income-Eligible Reduced Fares” or “IERF.” Initial development, design,
and approval of the program was funded by a $5,000,000 allocation in the FY 2024 Final Budget1
and the launch of this program was funded by a $20,000,000 allocation in the FY 2025 Final
Budget.2
The MBTA is pleased to offer this early report on adoption and impact of the first months of the
program. Data is provided as of March 31, 2025 (consistent with the language in Section 2F of
Chapter 140 of the Acts of 2024) with some additional updates through August 2025.
Program Overview
The MBTA’s mission is to serve the public by providing safe, reliable, and accessible transportation.
We are proud of the recent improvements in service quality and the more than 225 million trips the
Authority provided in the last fiscal year, but we know that fares can sometimes be a barrier to
accessibility for riders with limited incomes.
Historically, lower reduced fares had only been available for young riders, Seniors, riders with
disabilities, and certain young adult riders with limited income. The MBTA did not historically provide
broad-based reduced fares for all riders with limited income, on all modes. Nor did the MBTA offer
any reduced fares on The RIDE paratransit service. This changed with the launch of the IERF

are too young for Senior fares, providing them with credentials that unlock fares of at least 50% off

The IERF program enrollment and administration platform was built as a joint effort across multiple
Commonwealth agency partners: the MBTA, the Registry of Motor Vehicles (RMV), the
Massachusetts Department of Transportation (MassDOT), the Executive Office of Health and Human
Services (EOHHS), and the Department of Transitional Assistance (DTA). Experience with previous
means-tested programs – both within the MBTA and across various social service agencies – has
shown that administrative burden can create barriers to program uptake and usage both on the part
of the entities that run the programs and, more importantly, the individuals that apply to use those
benefits. The MBTA and our partner agencies designed a platform to minimize and largely avoid
many of these barriers by:
• Leveraging existing enrollment in other means-tested programs as proof of eligibility, rather
than standing up an IERF-specific eligibility verification process. These qualifying programs
include:
o Emergency Aid to the Elderly, Disabled & Children (EAEDC)
o MASSGrant
o MassHealth CarePlus
o MassHealth Family Assistance
o MassHealth Limited
o MassHealth Standard
o Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP)
o Transitional Assistance for Families and Dependent Children (TAFDC)
• Relying on our partners at RMV to verify applicant identity through their database of driver’s
licenses and other Massachusetts state IDs.
o Note that Massachusetts state IDs and enrollment in select EOHHS programs are
available to Massachusetts residents which was an important component to the
system design.
• Prioritizing an online, automated, database-driven process over one that relies on in-person,
manual application and processing to reduce administrative burdens and costs.
o The MBTA established novel Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) that enable
direct database connections across MBTA, RMV, and EOHHS platforms, allowing
instant identity and eligibility verification across agencies with no manual intervention
required.
• Still providing in-person application options and support to those for whom applying online is
not the best option but relying on established existing non-profit organizations to fulfill this
role rather than hiring significant internal staff and/or standing up new dedicated offices and
locations.
o The MBTA partnered with Action for Boston Community Development (ABCD) and the
Massachusetts Association for Community Action (MASSCAP) network to integrate

IERF support into their existing suite of services long provided in the community,

The end result of this novel program design is an online-first application and account management
platform that auto-approves approximately 70% of eligible applicants, instantly, in a process that
takes under five minutes to complete from anywhere. An additional approximately 25% of eligible
applicants are not auto-approved but are still approved via online application after submitting photos
of their relevant documents. Between these two methods, more than 90% of eligible applicants are
approved online without ever having to leave their houses or show up to an office or appointment.
The final 5 - 10% of approved applicants apply in-person at an ABCD or MASSCAP location located
throughout the MBTA service area, or at the MBTA’s Charlie Service Center in Downtown Boston.
The application process is fast, easy, and flexible, and has resulted in immediate positive impacts for
MBTA riders. In a recent customer service survey, 95% of responding riders said they were either
satisfied or very satisfied with the application process.
Analysis of program enrollment data also demonstrates program usage and uptake benefits of the
MBTA’s novel application process. For example, prior to IERF, the MBTA administered a “Youth Pass”
program that provided reduced fares to income-eligible young adults up to the age of 25. The Youth
Pass program had largely the same eligibility requirements as IERF but was administered on a less
convenient municipality-based basis. It served approximately 6,900 riders. When IERF launched in
September 2024 the new program quickly enrolled 6,900 riders under the age of 25 in
approximately the first month – i.e., the population formerly enrolled in Youth Pass. However,
additional income-eligible young adults beyond the initial 6,900 then continued to enroll in the
program every month, totaling more than 10,000 by Summer 2025. These additional young adult
enrollees did not gain access to reduced fares with IERF’s passage– they were largely already
qualified via Youth Pass – but they did enroll in IERF despite not previously enrolling in Youth Pass.
This is a testament to the benefits of IERF’s program design and implementation relative to prior
means-tested programs.
The MBTA is proud of the novel program design which has been built with cross-Secretariate and
community (?) support. This design has also been noticed by other major transit agencies and serves
as a model for other means-tested fare programs across the country. MBTA staff regularly receive
inquiries from other peer transit agencies, non-profit organizations, and industry researchers seeking
to learn from the success of IERF, the cross-agency partnerships it is built upon, and the program’s
novel API-based digital platform.
In addition to our program design and implementation, the MBTA is also proud of the benefits to
riders that IERF provides. Thanks to IERF and its corresponding expansion of reduced fares, income-
eligible Massachusetts residents of any age now have access to reduced fares across all of the
MBTA’s services. This benefit is not limited to any specific mode, routes, municipality, or other factor.
Reduced fare riders have access to, for example, Local Bus trips for $0.85 as opposed to $1.70,
subway trips for $1.10 as opposed to $2.40, and unlimited monthly bus and subway passes for $30
as opposed to $90. Single-ride tickets and passes for reduced fare riders are similarly discounted by
at least 50% on Commuter Rail and ferry, providing savings of up to $198 per month for a Commuter

The IERF program and its corresponding expansion of reduced fares also makes The RIDE more

Academic research consistently shows that access to affordable transportation provides social
benefits to those who use it. For example, a previous MIT study3 showed that riders who received
reduced fares took 30% more trips on the MBTA and specifically took more transit trips to health
care and social service providers. With the launch of the IERF program, the MBTA is proud to
facilitate these social impacts and looks forward to continued expansion of the program to increase
mobility for our lower-income riders.

Expected IERF Program Impacts: Short-Term Versus Long-Term
It is important to put the early progress of the IERF program in context with the program’s expected
impact over a multi-year period. Prior to the launch of the IERF program, MBTA staff generated
projected “uptake curves” of program enrollment and forecasts of program usage and impact. All of
these forward-looking estimates assumed that it would take approximately five years of ramp up
before the IERF program reached its “steady state” levels of enrollment, usage, and (inflation-
adjusted) costs. These estimates were based on ramp up experienced by other new means-tested
programs both in Massachusetts and across the country, from both transit and non-transit contexts.
In short, while a program like list can be launched in one year, it may take many years to educate
the population, encourage their engagement, and ultimately have people apply for and use the
benefit to which they have become entitled.

projected to be “induced” by the lower fares provided by IERF on an annual basis; meaning the

Program Impact on THE RIDE Paratransit Service
Reduced fares were first expanded to The RIDE in September 2024 and by Spring 2025 the majority
of all RIDE trips were provided at reduced fare prices. As a result of the significant fare decrease
within this mode and the unique cost structure of paratransit service, the cost of providing service for
trips on The RIDE “induced” by lower fares is expected to be significant. However, these costs have
not yet been calculated.
Here it is important to clarify how The RIDE’s operations differ from the MBTA’s other services.
Transit service on the MBTA’s fixed route modes (bus, subway, Commuter Rail, and ferry) is relatively
“scalable” and capable of absorbing some amount of increased demand without necessitating
increased service. While increased ridership beyond a certain level will require more bus, train, or
boat trips to be run, the quantity of induced trips forecast to be generated by IERF are not expected
to necessitate a major expansion of service, especially in the near term. Each bus or train generally
has the capacity to support some additional riders, until a tipping point of too many new riders is hit,
and we need to add another bus or train to the schedule.
The cost structure for providing service on The RIDE, however, is different. The personalized and
largely one-to-one nature of paratransit service – door-to-door trips in smaller vehicles (cars or vans)
dispatched to serve passengers at specific times – makes total costs of providing service on this
mode highly variable and tightly tied to ridership or calls for a trip. This also results in higher costs on
a per-passenger basis, with each additional trip costing upwards of $70 to provide.
The MBTA also has legal obligations under the ADA to ensure adequate and timely paratransit
service to a degree which does not apply to the MBTA’s fixed route modes. If a bus rider cannot
board a vehicle due to crowding and is left behind at the curb, that is an unfortunate and
unacceptable failure of service. However, if a paratransit customer is not served by The RIDE and
gets left behind at the curb, that is a severe violation of federal civil rights law.
Accordingly, MBTA staff projected the annual cost of serving the induced demand generated by
reduced fares on The RIDE to be significant, ranging from approximately $16 to $21 million per year.
In fact, paratransit operational costs are predicted to be the largest category of the total “costs” of
this program in the early period following launch. Paratransit operational costs are expected to then

MBTA staff are currently analyzing RIDE cost and passenger demand data to calculate actual
induced-demand service costs incurred to date, but this analysis is still ongoing and results are not
yet available. The fluctuating costs of providing RIDE service are impacted by many other factors in
addition to the fares charged to riders. Notably, the MBTA has been undertaking a series of initiatives
to improve service for our paratransit customers concurrently with the launch of the IERF program. It
is proving challenging to appropriately isolate the direct impact of the expansion of reduced fares –
versus some of these others service improvements – on service costs on this mode to date. The
MBTA recently on-boarded a new analyst, adding capacity to research and resolve these complex
funding allocation questions. This is an area of great interest to the MBTA, and future research into
this topic is a high priority for MBTA analysts and policymakers. In future budget updates, the MBTA
anticipates having a viable methodology to determine a proper cost-allocation of increasing
paratransit costs as part of the broader IERF program.
