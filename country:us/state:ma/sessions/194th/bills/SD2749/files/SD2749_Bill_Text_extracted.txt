Chapter 126 of the Acts of 2023 line item 4000-0950 requires as follows:
[T]he secretary of health and human services shall report biannually to the house and
senate committees on ways and means on the implementation of the initiative; provided
further, that said reports shall include, but not be limited to: (i) the results of the
scheduled plan to date, including a schedule detailing commencement of services and
associated costs by service type; (ii) an analysis of compliance with the terms of the
settlement agreement to date; (iii) a detailed itemization of services and service utilization
by service type, geographical location and the age of the member receiving the service;
(iv) data detailing the time that elapsed between a member’s request for services and
commencement of an initial assessment for services; (v) the time to complete the initial
assessment and the time that elapsed between initial assessment for services and
commencement of services; and (vi) a quarterly update of whether projected expenditures
are likely to exceed the amount appropriated in this item[.]
MassHealth is pleased to report that, pursuant to an order dated June 19, 2021, the judgment in
the case of Rosie D. v. Baker was terminated after full implementation of the remedial order.

In State Fiscal Year 2023, approximately 26,1341 unduplicated members received Children’s
Behavioral Health Initiative (CBHI) services. Many members utilize more than one CBHI
service.
Since 2010, providers have been paid $2.85 billion to deliver CBHI services as follows:
• $541 million to deliver Intensive Care Coordination (ICC),
• $193 million to deliver In-Home Behavioral Services (IHBS),
• $1.4 billion to deliver In-Home Therapy (IHT),
• $141 million to deliver Family Support and Training (FS&T),
• $172 million to deliver Mobile Crisis Intervention (MCI), and
• $549 million to deliver Therapeutic Mentor (TM)
Additionally, MassHealth has spent approximately $72 million to providers to administer the
Children and Adolescents Needs and Strengths (CANS) assessment.
Since July 2010, the average wait time to obtain Intensive Care Coordination is 12.9 days, and
over half of the youth seeking Intensive Care Coordination began treatment within 3 days. Since
2010, 75% of youth waiting for Intensive Care Coordination for any period of time began
services within 14 days of being referred to the service; 81% began services within 21 days; and
85% began services within 28 days.
Additionally, MassHealth reports that, based on the information available regarding expenditures
to-date, we project expenditures for this line item for FY24 will be within the appropriated
amount of $315,282,721.
I am grateful for your continued support of the MassHealth program. Please feel free to contact
Sarah Nordberg at Sarah.Nordberg@mass.gov should you have any questions about this report.
