Dear Members of the General Court:
I am pleased to submit this Report to the Legislature: Genocide Education Trust Fund
and Programming: FY2025. The Department of Elementary and Secondary Education’s
genocide education work is based on Chapter 98 of the Acts of 2021, An act concerning
genocide education, which directs the Department to establish a grant program to
support instruction on the history of genocide at the middle and high school levels.
Chapter 98 also established a Genocide Education Trust Fund for the purposes of
educating middle and high school students on the history of genocide and to assist in
promoting the teaching of human rights issues in all public schools and school districts,
with particular attention to the study of the inhumanity of genocide.
This report is pursuant to Chapter 98, which stipulates:
“…the commissioner of elementary and secondary education shall report to the
clerks of the house of representatives and the senate, the joint committee on
education and the house and senate committees on ways and means on the
fund’s activities. The report shall include, but not be limited to: (i) the source and
amount of funds received; (ii) the expenditures made from the fund and the
purposes of such expenditures; (iii) any grants provided to relevant community
stakeholders and other stakeholder organizations; (iv) anticipated revenue and
expenditure projections for the next fiscal year; (v) the number of schools and
school districts that have used the fund to implement a new program or enhance
or maintain current programming; and (vi) the number of schools and school
districts that applied for, but were not granted, funding. The report shall be
publicly available on the department’s website.”
Chapter 98 also requires that:
“…the commissioner shall provide a report on the progress of genocide
education programs in public schools and school districts to the clerks of
the house of representatives and the senate and the joint committee on
education. The report shall provide a description of the manner in which
grantees…are offering genocide instruction including: (i) the number of
hours of instruction offered; (ii) the grade levels at which such instruction
is offered; and (iii) the title and description of the course in which such
instruction is integrated.”

During FY2025, the Department continued to offer the Genocide Education Grant,
which provided 42 Local Education Agencies with the funds to expand and deepen their
instruction in the history and patterns of genocide. In this report, we summarize findings
based on the participation of these LEAs in a statewide evaluation of genocide
education as well as a review of their grant applications.
As we move into FY2026, the need for education to combat hate and recognize the
mistakes of the past has continued to be urgent. With the Legislature’s allocation of $3
million to the Genocide Education Trust Fund in FY2026, the Department is grateful to
be able to provide additional grant funding and resources to further this important
work.
Sincerely,
Pedro Martinez
Commissioner of Elementary and Secondary Education

Introduction
The Department of Elementary and Secondary Education (Department) respectfully
submits this Report to the Legislature: Genocide Education Trust Fund: FY2025,
pursuant to Chapter 98 of the Acts of 2021, which stipulates:
“There is hereby established and set up on the books of the
commonwealth a separate fund to be known as the Genocide Education
Trust Fund for the purpose of educating middle and high school students
on the history of genocide…the commissioner of elementary and
secondary education shall report to the clerks of the house of
representatives and the senate, the joint committee on education and the
house and senate committees on ways and means on the fund’s
activities.”
As stated above, the Genocide Education Trust Fund is intended to strengthen
instruction on the history of genocide for middle and high school students. More
specifically, Chapter 98 requires that every school district, to educate middle and high
school students, provide instruction on the history of genocide consistent with the
content standards articulated in the History/Social Science Curriculum Framework.
In FY2025, the state legislature appropriated $3 million for the Genocide Education
Trust Fund to strengthen instruction in the history and patterns of genocide. Per Chapter
98, the Genocide Education Trust Fund may be used:
• to develop curricular materials detailing the underlying causes, international
reaction, progression, and aftermath of genocide; and
• to provide and supply professional development trainings, seminars,
conferences, and materials, for educators to use in the teaching of genocide.
The Trust Fund may also be used for other purposes related to instructing middle and
high school students on the history of genocide, as long as at least 50 percent of the
funds expended annually are allocated to public schools or school districts.
During FY2025, the Trust Fund was used to offer the Genocide Education Grant. This
grant provided 42 Local Education Agencies with the funds to expand and deepen their
instruction in the history and patterns of genocide. In addition, funds were used to
support an evaluation of the Genocide Education Grant and to conduct a landscape
analysis of genocide education instruction across Massachusetts.

Detailed Report on FY2025 Trust Fund Spending
Grant Awards
FC215/FC215a: Genocide Education Grant
Massachusetts LEAs were eligible to apply for Cycle 2 funding in FY2024 (FC 215a),
with awardees having the option to divide their funds between FY2024 and FY2025.
Those recommended for funding most closely met the criteria outlined in the Funding
Opportunity RFP document. 43 LEAs were awarded funds across FY2024 and FY2025,
with 42 receiving funds for FY2025. Four proposals were not funded due to incomplete
information or a lack of alignment with the priorities of the RFP.
The grant was intended to support LEAs in strengthening instruction in the history and
patterns of genocide. Priority was given to LEAs with schools requiring assistance
according to the state accountability system and/or where greater than 40 percent of the
student population was designated as low-income. Grant applications were also
prioritized based on:
1. Aligned, meaningful learning opportunities: Proposals that aligned materials,
curriculum, professional development, and/or experiential learning opportunities
to the History and Social Science Framework and the goals outlined in Chapter
98 of the Acts of 2021, An act concerning genocide education. Meaningful

learning experiences that were well-integrated into existing instruction were
prioritized.
2. Equity: Proposals that increased equitable access to high-quality genocide
education for all students were preferred.
3. Sustainability: Proposals that led to sustainable, long-term impact were
preferred over “one-off” opportunities.
4. Partnership: Preferred proposals included opportunities to collaborate with
relevant organizations and/or engage local community members.
Ultimately, the funded projects included several activities such as partnering with
external organizations to conduct professional development for teachers, supporting
teams of history teachers in incorporating high-quality instruction on genocide into their
existing curriculum, and taking field trips to relevant destinations. The Department
awarded $1,204,192, and approximately $1,138,321 was spent in FY2025.
A list of grant recipients can be found in Appendix A.
Project Expenses
Administrative Expenses, Pension & Insurance Related Expenses, Special Employees
To expand the Department’s capacity to support educators with Genocide Education, a
contractor was hired to manage the Genocide Education grant cycle and evaluation.
Associated expenses, as well as indirect cost expenditures, were $102,742.19 in FY25.
Consultant Services
In partnership with research organization MC2 Education, the Department continued a
genocide education evaluation. This statewide study includes an evaluation of the
Genocide Education Grant as well as a landscape analysis of the current state of
genocide education instruction across the state. In FY2025, total expenditures for this
project were $908,196.75.
The Department also contracted with Harvard University to provide professional
development to history and social science teachers and leaders, at a cost of
$396,728.97. Finally, the Department worked with multiple contracted vendors to
produce the Investigating History curriculum, which is used in hundreds of middle
school classrooms across the state and addresses genocide alongside other topics as
indicated by the History and Social Science Curriculum Framework. $41,193.00 from
the Genocide Education Trust Fund was used towards this project.

Looking Ahead: Major FY2026 Expenditures
In FY2026, the Department will continue to fund the genocide education evaluation,
specifically for grant monitoring. The Department will begin to use Genocide Education
Trust Fund funds to design additional supports and resources for genocide education.
Alongside these projects, the Department plans to continue offering grant funding to
support districts’ efforts to strengthen genocide education. Additionally, the Department
will continue to employ a contract employee to coordinate all these projects.
Grantees’ Genocide Education Programming in
FY2025
The remainder of this report will describe genocide education instruction offered by
recipients of the Genocide Education Grant, including:
• the number of hours of instruction offered,
• the grade levels at which such instruction is offered, and
• the title and description of the course in which such instruction is integrated.
All FY25 grantees received a survey from research organization MC2 Education as part
of a Department-sponsored statewide evaluation of genocide education. Of those grant
recipients, 24 (57%) responded to the survey. However, not every grantee provided all
the requested information.
Grade Levels
All grantee respondents (20) offered instruction in topics related to genocide across all
secondary grade levels, with the most frequent instruction occurring in grades 9, 10,
and 11. Responding grantees indicated offering an average of 2.6 courses across the
LEA that included instruction in genocide.

Instructional Hours
Of those that reported instructional hours, LEAs offered an average of 95.6 hours
related to genocide education.
Courses and Topics of Instruction
LEAs offered instruction in genocide across a variety of courses, including both required
and elective courses. 98% of responding grantees reported including topics of genocide
within mandatory courses in history and social studies. Instruction was most frequently
offered in required high school ELA and history courses (US History I/II and World
History I/II).

Summary
Through its work in FY2025, the Department has leveraged the Genocide Education
Trust Fund to continue opportunities for teachers and students to learn vitally important
history to support students’ development and ability to confront injustice and make
informed decisions. As evidenced by the data shared in this report, LEAs have
continued to take advantage of these opportunities to expand and deepen their
instruction surrounding the history and patterns of genocide. We look forward to
continuing this work in FY2026.
