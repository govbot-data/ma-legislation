Dear Members of the General Court:
I am pleased to submit this Report to the Legislature: Genocide Education Trust Fund and
Programming: FY2024. The Department of Elementary and Secondary Education’s
genocide education work is based on Chapter 98 of the Acts of 2021, An act concerning
genocide education, which directs the Department to establish a grant program to support
instruction on the history of genocide at the middle and high school levels. Chapter 98 also
established a Genocide Education Trust Fund for the purposes of educating middle and
high school students on the history of genocide and to assist in promoting the teaching of
human rights issues in all public schools and school districts, with particular attention to
the study of the inhumanity of genocide.
This report is pursuant to Chapter 98, which stipulates:
“…the commissioner of elementary and secondary education shall report to the
clerks of the house of representatives and the senate, the joint committee on
education and the house and senate committees on ways and means on the fund’s
activities. The report shall include, but not be limited to: (i) the source and amount
of funds received; (ii) the expenditures made from the fund and the purposes of
such expenditures; (iii) any grants provided to relevant community stakeholders and
other stakeholder organizations; (iv) anticipated revenue and expenditure
projections for the next fiscal year; (v) the number of schools and school districts
that have used the fund to implement a new program or enhance or maintain
current programming; and (vi) the number of schools and school districts that
applied for, but were not granted, funding. The report shall be publicly available on
the department’s website.”
Chapter 98 also requires that:
“…the commissioner shall provide a report on the progress of genocide
education programs in public schools and school districts to the clerks of the
house of representatives and the senate and the joint committee on
education. The report shall provide a description of the manner in which
grantees…are offering genocide instruction including: (i) the number of hours
of instruction offered; (ii) the grade levels at which such instruction is offered;

and (iii) the title and description of the course in which such instruction is
integrated.”
During FY2024, the Department continued to offer the Genocide Education Grant, which
provided 51 Local Education Agencies with the funds to expand and deepen their
instruction in the history and patterns of genocide. In this report, we summarize findings
based on the participation of these LEAs in a statewide evaluation of genocide education
as well as a review of their grant applications.
As we move into FY2025, the need for education to combat hate and recognize the
mistakes of the past has only become more urgent. With the Legislature’s allocation of $3
million to the Genocide Education Trust Fund, I am excited to be able to provide additional
grant funding and resources to further this important work.
Sincerely,
Russell D. Johnston
Acting Commissioner of Elementary and Secondary Education

Introduction
The Department of Elementary and Secondary Education (Department) respectfully
submits this Report to the Legislature: Genocide Education Trust Fund: FY2024, pursuant
to Chapter 98 of the Acts of 2021, which stipulates:
“There is hereby established and set up on the books of the commonwealth a
separate fund to be known as the Genocide Education Trust Fund for the
purpose of educating middle and high school students on the history of
genocide…the commissioner of elementary and secondary education shall
report to the clerks of the house of representatives and the senate, the joint
committee on education and the house and senate committees on ways and
means on the fund’s activities.”
As stated above, the Genocide Education Trust Fund is intended to strengthen instruction
on the history of genocide for middle and high school students. More specifically, Chapter
98 requires that every school district, to educate middle and high school students, provide
instruction on the history of genocide consistent with the content standards articulated in
the History/Social Science Curriculum Framework.
In FY2024, the state legislature appropriated $2 million for the Genocide Education Trust
Fund to strengthen instruction in the history and patterns of genocide. Per Chapter 98, the
Genocide Education Trust Fund may be used:
• to develop curricular materials detailing the underlying causes, international
reaction, progression, and aftermath of genocide; and
• to provide and supply professional development trainings, seminars, conferences,
and materials, for educators to use in the teaching of genocide.
The Trust Fund may also be used for other purposes related to instructing middle and high
school students on the history of genocide, as long as at least 50 percent of the funds
expended annually are allocated to public schools or school districts.
During FY2024, the Trust Fund was used to offer the Genocide Education Grant. This grant
provided 51 Local Education Agencies with the funds to expand and deepen their
instruction in the history and patterns of genocide. In addition, funds were used to support
a curriculum review of available genocide education materials. Finally, funds were used to
support an evaluation of the Genocide Education Grant and to conduct a landscape
analysis of genocide education instruction across Massachusetts.

Detailed Report on FY2024 Trust Fund Spending
Grant Awards
FC 215/FC 215a: Genocide Education Grant
For FY2024, 36 LEAs were awarded Cycle 1 Genocide Education Grant funds as a
continuation of work they began in FY2023. In addition, Massachusetts LEAs were eligible
to apply for Cycle 2 funding in FY2024, with awardees having the option to divide their
funds between FY2024 and FY2025. Those recommended for funding most closely met the
criteria outlined in the Funding Opportunity RFP document. 43 LEAs were awarded funds
across FY2024 and FY2025, with 16 receiving funds for FY2024. Four proposals were not
funded due to incomplete information or lack of alignment with the priorities of the RFP.
The grant was intended to support LEAs in strengthening instruction in the history and
patterns of genocide. Priority was given to LEAs with schools requiring assistance
according to the state accountability system and/or where greater than 40 percent of the
student population was designated as low-income. Grant applications were also prioritized
based on:
1. Aligned, meaningful learning opportunities: Proposals that aligned materials,
curriculum, professional development, and/or experiential learning opportunities to
the History and Social Science Framework and the goals outlined in Chapter 98 of
the Acts of 2021, An act concerning genocide education. Meaningful learning
experiences that were well-integrated into existing instruction were prioritized.
2. Equity: Proposals that increased equitable access to high-quality genocide
education for all students were preferred.
3

3. Sustainability: Proposals that led to sustainable, long-term impact were preferred
over “one-off” opportunities.
4. Partnership: Preferred proposals included opportunities to collaborate with
relevant organizations and/or engage local community members.
Ultimately, the funded projects included several activities such as partnering with external
organizations to conduct professional development for teachers, supporting teams of
history teachers in incorporating high-quality instruction on genocide into their existing
curriculum, and taking field trips to relevant destinations. LEAs selected in Cycle 1 were
awarded $691,189 for FY2024 as a continuation of work they began in FY2023. In addition,
the Department awarded $1,343,089 in Cycle 2 funding. Across both grants, $762,583 was
spent in FY2024 and approximately $1,204,192 will be in FY2025.
A list of recipients for Cycle 1 and Cycle 2 can be found in Appendix A.
Project Expenses
Curriculum Review
In fall 2023, the Department published the K-12 History Social Science Curricular Materials
Guide, a synthesis of several review projects developed in partnership with the Rennie
Center for Education Research and Policy. The guide contains information for
Massachusetts educators and district leaders selecting history/social science curricular
materials at grades K-5, grade 8, and grades 9-12 levels. As part of the grades 9-12 review,
the Department included an evaluation of supplemental materials intended to support
instruction about genocide. In FY2024, total expenditures for this project were $37,292.
Genocide Education Evaluation
In partnership with research organization MC2 Education, the Department began a
genocide education evaluation. This statewide study includes an evaluation of the
Genocide Education Grant as well as a landscape analysis of the current state of genocide
education instruction across the state. In FY2024, total expenditures for this project were
$114,419.
Looking Ahead: Major FY2025 Expenditures
In FY2025, the Department will continue to fund the genocide education evaluation,
completing the landscape analysis phase of this study. The Department will use the
findings from this evaluation to design additional supports and resources for genocide
education, which will also be funded by the Genocide Education Trust Fund. Additionally,
the Department has hired a contract employee to increase capacity for this work.
Alongside these projects, the Department plans to continue offering grant funding to
support districts’ efforts to strengthen genocide education.

Grantees’ Genocide Education Programming in FY2024
The remainder of this report will describe genocide education instruction offered by
recipients of the Genocide Education Grant, including:
• the number of hours of instruction offered,
• the grade levels at which such instruction is offered, and
• the title and description of the course in which such instruction is integrated.
It summarizes findings based on the responses of the two groups of grantees described
above:
• Cycle 1 Grantees: The 39 LEAs awarded grants for FY2023 and FY2024. Of those,
three received awards for FY2023 only. Two pairs of two LEAs each received joint
awards.
• Cycle 2 Grantees: The 16 LEAs newly awarded grants for FY2024. Of these, 14 were
also awarded grants to extend activities into FY2025.
As part of Cycle 2, 26 additional recipients were awarded grants for FY2025 only. Their
information is not summarized in this report.
Due to the timing of grant awards, methods of data collection differed across Cycle 1
Grantees and Cycle 2 Grantees.
The 39 Cycle 1 Grantees received a survey from research organization MC2 Education as
part of a Department-sponsored statewide evaluation of genocide education. Of those
grant recipients, 34 (87%) responded to the survey. However, not every grantee provided all
the requested information.
For the 16 Cycle 2 Grantees, information was gathered from grant applications only. 15 out
of 16 grantees reported course information.

Cycle 2 Grantees
Cycle 2 LEAs also offered instruction in genocide across several courses, including both
required and elective courses. 87.5% of grantees reported including topics of genocide
within mandatory courses in history, social studies, and English Language Arts. Instruction
was most frequently offered in required high school history courses (US History I/II, World

• 62% of grantees reported offering electives that included genocide education

Summary
Through its work in FY2024, the Department has leveraged the Genocide Education Trust
Fund to create new opportunities for teachers and students to learn vitally important
history to support their development as ethical, informed young people with the knowledge
and frameworks to confront injustices and make moral choices. As evidenced by the data
shared in this report, LEAs have taken advantage of these opportunities to expand and
deepen their instruction surrounding the history and patterns of genocide. We look forward
to continuing this work in FY2025.
