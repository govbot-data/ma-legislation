Executive Summary
The Housing Choice Initiative (“HCI”) started in 2017 to encourage housing production by
recognizing municipalities permitting certain thresholds of net new housing units and adopting certain
pro-housing best practices. The initiative is built around a Housing Choice Community Designation (“the
Designation”), an exclusive grant program, and other key policy tools such as reforms to MGL c. 40A (“the
Zoning Act”), including simple majority quantum of vote for “pro-housing” zoning amendments and M.G.L.
c40A s3A, the MBTA Communities Law and its Regulations. This report covers data about the initiative
from its inception in 2017 until the 2024 round of designations and the FY25 round of grant awards.
The Designation recognizes communities that have made strides to create more housing and
implement local pro-housing policies. It also provides Housing Choice Communities (“HCC”) exclusive
access to the Housing Choice Grant Program (“HCGP”) through the Community One Stop for Growth (“One
Stop”) and special considerations to HCI participating state programs, such as the Clean Water Trust1.
Since the inception of the Housing Choice Initiative, 112 communities have received the Designation and,
in 2024, 98 communities were designated as Housing Choice Communities and were eligible for all
benefits of the program. The HCGP makes competitive grants available for HCCs of up to $500,000 in
capital funding for a variety of projects that create and support housing and invest in vital local needs. As
of FY25, the program awarded 212 grants to eligible communities totaling $32,616,070 in state
investments for local initiatives that support housing.
Since the implementation of the HCI, the Designation, and the HCGP, 78% of all housing
produced in the Commonwealth occurred in Housing Choice communities. The HCGP has directly
created an estimated 1,423 units and supported an estimated 7,318 units with state investment from the
program. As the HCI looks to the future, EOHLC will continue to encourage housing growth and the
implementation of best practices, invest in housing production, support existing housing, make
investments in infrastructure and planning and zoning projects, and continue to provide assistance to
MBTA Communities.
The Healey-Driscoll Administration has identified affordable and abundant housing as a priority
and remains focused on promoting strategies to advance housing production, in partnership with the
Legislature, including through the Affordable Homes Act (2024 Mass. Acts, Chapter 150), to strengthen
our Commonwealth’s ability to build more housing, tackle our affordability needs, and create greater
housing choices in all communities for all people.
Housing Choice Designation
As part of the HCI, municipalities that have produced certain numbers of housing units in the last
five years and/or have adopted a minimum number of “pro-housing” best practices are eligible to receive
the Designation. All Cities and Towns throughout the Commonwealth of Massachusetts are eligible to
apply to become an HCC. Those that receive the Designation have exclusive access to the HCGP2 through
the One Stop3 and receive bonus points or other considerations to nine (9) HCI participating state

Grants Awarded
As part of the Housing Choice Initiative, the grant program rewards HCCs for their efforts to
produce housing units and promotes projects that will further housing production in each HCC. The grant
program funds a wide array of activities related to community planning and zoning, feasibility,
engineering, design, and construction of physical infrastructure and of existing and new buildings,
rehabilitation, due diligence, plan designs, market studies, pre-permitting/permitting, and engineering,
acquisition, demolition, and site-related upgrades of future development sites. As a tool to reward
communities for the housing they have produced, the grant program has both supported and helped to
create housing in HCCs12. The HCGP was reauthorized in the Affordable Homes Act (2024 Mass. Acts,
c.150, § 2A, line item 7004-0082).13
To date, the Housing Choice Grant Program has awarded 212 grants totaling $32,616,070. Of
those, 162 grants were awarded to MBTA Communities totaling $25,563,992.00 and, out of a “set aside”
for Rural and Small Towns for FY19 through FY21 award rounds, 28 grants were awarded to small towns
totaling $2,395,174 in awards (Table 2)14. Overall, to date, 49 grants have been awarded to Rural and/or
Small Towns totaling $4,915,878 in awards. The minimum grant awarded was $20,000 and the maximum
for the cap of $500,000, with a median grant award of $153,850. In FY24, we increased the grant cap to
$500,000 to better meet local needs.

The grant cycle opens as part of the Community One Stop for Growth in May of each year, with
all applications due by the start of June. These grants either “support” or “create” housing. Generally
speaking, “support” indicates projects that address the infrastructure or planning and zoning goals of an
HCC and that address the needs around current or new housing (e.g., a sidewalk connecting a new/existing
40B project to a village center), and “create” indicates projects that help HCCs unlock opportunities to
build housing that, without the grant, would otherwise not exist, be financially unfeasible, or take longer
(e.g., funding engineering designs for a new septic system for a municipality to build housing). Appendix
2 includes a list of FY25 Housing Choice grants with a brief description. Appendix 3 summarizes the most
common categories of all past grant activities. Within the category of creating housing, from FY19-25 the
program helped create an estimated 1,423 units; within the category of supporting housing, the program
supported an estimated 7,319 units.
Progress to Housing Production Goal of 135,000 new units
Per a statutory requirement in Section 101 of Session Law Acts of 2020 Chapter 358, EOHLC has
been tracking progress made towards the production of 135,0000 new units between 2018 and 202515.
The Housing Choice Initiative has utilized the U.S. Census Building Permit Survey to measure housing
production in the Commonwealth. Despite possible undercounts16, this is currently the best metric to
measure housing production across the Commonwealth. Since 2018 through 2024, the U.S. Census
Building Permit Survey reports that Massachusetts has built a total of 116,507 units. Since 2018, 78% of
all housing produced in the Commonwealth was produced by a community designated as a Housing
Choice Community at some point through the existence of the initiative.17
