1 of 12

13 Section 1E 1/2. (a) As used in this section and section 1E 3/4, the following words shall,
14 unless the context clearly requires otherwise, have the following meanings:
15 “Evidence-based literacy instruction”, literacy instruction that is: (i) grounded in
16 scientific research methods; and (ii) demonstrated through well-designed and well-implemented
17 studies to produce significant and positive effects on student learning outcomes.
18 “Five research-based areas in reading instruction”, reading instruction that includes, but
19 shall not be limited to, phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary and comprehension.
20 “High-quality curriculum”, curriculum reviewed and approved by the department.
21 (b) No curricula selected to meet a curriculum framework pursuant to section 1E for
22 kindergarten through grade 3 literacy shall include implicit and incidental instruction in word
23 reading, visual memorization of whole words, guessing from context and picture cues, also
24 known as MSV or three-cueing; provided, that curricula selected to meet a curriculum
25 framework for kindergarten through grade 3 literacy may use pictures as a supplement to support
26 reading and literacy; and provided further, that said curriculum that uses pictures shall be in
27 compliance with subsection (c).
28 (c) Districts shall select high-quality curricula to meet the curriculum frameworks
29 pursuant to section 1E for kindergarten through grade 3 literacy that: (i) include instruction in the
30 five research-based areas in reading instruction; and (ii) utilize and are based upon evidence-
31 based literacy instruction.
2 of 12

32 (d)(1) A district may apply to the department for a waiver to use kindergarten through
33 grade 3 literacy instruction curriculum that does not appear on the department’s list of high-
34 quality curricula.
35 (2) In determining whether to approve a district’s waiver for a curriculum that is not
36 already on the department’s list of high-quality curricula, the department shall assess whether the
37 district’s curriculum utilizes evidence-based literacy instruction.
38 (e)(1) A district may apply to the department for a waiver to use a kindergarten through
39 grade 3 literacy instruction curriculum pilot program that utilizes curricula that the department
40 has not yet reviewed to determine if it is evidence-based literacy instruction.
41 (2) A district shall report to the department: (i) the degree to which the pilot’s
42 instructional approach is supported by some degree of scientific research; (ii) the methodology of
43 the pilot; (iii) the population that was instructed using the pilot curriculum and any demographic
44 differences between that population and the district’s student body at large; (iv) the duration of
45 any such pilot; and (v) any other considerations that the department deems relevant to the quality
46 and fairness of the pilot program’s execution and impact on student learning.
47 (3) In determining whether to approve a district’s application to participate in a pilot
48 program pursuant to this subsection, the department shall assess the quality of the proposed pilot
49 program, including, information received pursuant to paragraph (2).
50 (4) Curriculum for a pilot program that is assessed and approved by the department and
51 deemed to meet the requirements of this section shall be allowed to continue as an approved
52 literacy curriculum used for instruction in the district unless the pilot program is rescinded
53 pursuant to paragraph (7).
3 of 12

54 (5) A district that participates in a pilot program pursuant to this subsection shall publicly
55 post to its website any report submitted to the department with information required pursuant to
56 paragraph (2) and curriculum samples.
57 (6) The department may audit approved pilot programs at any time. The audit shall
58 include, but shall not be limited to: (i) an in-depth review of curriculum materials; (ii)
59 instructional documentation; and (iii) instructional practices in the classroom.
60 (7) The department may rescind approval of a pilot program based on audit findings
61 before the expiration of the pilot program.
62 (f) Annually, not later than October 31, each school district shall report to the department
63 the number of reading specialists, reading interventionists, literacy coordinators, school
64 librarians, library aides, education support professionals and other staff employed by the district
65 that directly support student reading and literacy development.
66 Section 1E ¾. (a) The department shall provide tools and resources to aid districts in
67 providing professional development aligned with evidence-based literacy instruction for literacy
68 teachers, paraprofessionals and reading specialists in kindergarten through grade 3.
69 (b) The department shall create and maintain:
70 (i) a list of department-approved professional development programs and vendors aligned
71 with evidence-based literacy instruction best practices; and
72 (ii) a set of online training modules available and free to all teachers that provide training
73 on the foundational practices and pedagogy aligned with evidence-based literacy instruction best
74 practices.

132 SECTION 10. Said chapter 71 is hereby further amended by inserting after section 57A
133 the following 2 sections:-
134 Section 57B. (a) For the purposes of this section and section 57C, the term “screening
135 assessments” shall mean tools and assessments that can be used to identify students at risk for
136 poor academic outcomes as determined by the department.
137 (b) The department, in consultation with the department of early education and care, shall
138 issue guidelines to districts requiring screening procedures and protocols for reading ability and
7 of 12

139 progress in literacy skills for all students in kindergarten through grade 3 using screening
140 assessments. The guidelines shall include a threshold at which a student is determined to be
141 “significantly below” the grade level benchmark. The commissioner shall annually publish data
142 on which screening assessments are employed by each district to conduct reading screening.
143 Section 57C. (a) Each district shall, not less than twice per year, assess each student’s
144 reading ability and progress in literacy skills from kindergarten through at least grade 3, using a
145 screening assessment approved by the department. The screening assessment of the student’s
146 reading ability and progress shall be consistent with section 2 of chapter 71B, the department’s
147 dyslexia guidelines issued pursuant to section 57A and literacy guidelines issued pursuant to
148 section 57B.
149 (b) If a screening assessment determines that a student is significantly below relevant
150 grade level benchmarks in specific literacy skills, the district shall determine which actions
151 within the general education program will meet the student’s needs, including, but not limited to,
152 differentiated or supplementary evidence-based reading instruction and ongoing monitoring of
153 progress. Not later than 30 days after a screening result that determines a student is significantly
154 below the relevant grade level benchmark, the district shall inform the student’s parent or
155 guardian of the screening results and the school’s response and shall offer the opportunity for a
156 follow-up discussion.

159 SECTION 290. (a) As used in this section, “high-needs district” shall mean a school or
160 district with a high percentage of low-income students and English learners, which may include a
161 school or district implementing turnaround plans.
162 (b) The department of elementary and secondary education shall, subject to
163 appropriation, develop and administer a pilot program, in partnership with a college, university,
164 school, district or other institution offering an educator preparation program approved pursuant
165 to section 38G of chapter 71 of the General Laws, for teacher apprenticeships during which
166 participants shall complete a paid teaching apprenticeship for school years in a high-needs
167 district in a classroom with a highly proficient mentor teacher. The department of elementary and
168 secondary education shall determine qualifications of a highly proficient mentor teacher using
169 factors, including, but not limited to, recent evaluation ratings, student growth data and
170 demonstrated evidence-based practices. The program shall provide funds to candidates to support
171 earning a competitive wage, including, but not limited to, support for childcare, transportation,
172 textbooks and other core expenses, to ensure a diverse cohort are able to fully participate.
173 (c) The department of elementary and secondary education shall promulgate regulations
174 defining the apprenticeship and the target candidates to be admitted to the program.
175 (d)(1) Not later than 1 year after the completion of the pilot program, the department of
176 elementary and secondary education shall file a report with the clerks of the house of
177 representatives and the senate and the joint committee on education on the outcome of the pilot
178 program. The report shall include, but shall not be limited to: (i) data on the demographics of
179 participants, participants’ licensure pass rates on the MTEL and hiring rates; and (ii) an
180 assessment of the feasibility of applying for status as a United States Registered Apprenticeship.
9 of 12

181 (2) Not later than 3 years after the completion of the pilot program, the department of
182 elementary and secondary education shall file a report with the clerks of the house of
183 representatives and the senate and the joint committee on education, which shall include, but
184 shall not be limited to, an evaluation of the impact of apprentices on student outcomes as
185 compared to teachers not participating in the pilot program using student growth measures and
186 an assessment of the feasibility of expanding to a teacher apprenticeship for all teacher
187 candidates or all candidates in high-needs districts.
