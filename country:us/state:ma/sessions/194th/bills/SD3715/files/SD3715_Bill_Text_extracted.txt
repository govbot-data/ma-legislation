REPORT OF THE DIVISION OF OCCUPATIONAL LICENSURE
TO THE HOUSE AND SENATE COMMITTEES ON WAYS AND MEANS
March 1, 2026
This report to the House and Senate Committees on Ways and Means is submitted by
the Division of Occupational Licensure (“DOL”) pursuant to section 35V of Chapter 10 of the
General Laws.
Section 35V establishes the Division of Occupational Licensure Trust Fund (“the DOL
Trust”). The DOL Trust is funded by revenue from licensure fees, and expenditures from the
DOL Trust are used for the administrative costs of operations and programs administered by the
agency.
Section 35V calls for the DOL Commissioner to submit an annual report on the following: the
revenue credited to the DOL Trust; the amount of DOL Trust expenditures that are attributable to
the shared administrative costs of DOL and an explanation of why such administrative costs are
necessary; an itemized list of the amount of trust funds expended by board or unit; and an
analysis of the services provided based on trust fund expenditures by board or unit, including the
manner in which trust fund expenditures assist DOL in meeting its regulatory mandates.
I. DOL BACKGROUND
The Division of Occupational Licensure, formerly known as the Division of Professional
Licensure, is located in the Office of Consumer Affairs and Business Regulation. Pursuant to the
authorities in Chapters 13 and 112 of the General Laws, DOL supervises and supports the
operations of 15 boards of registration and examination that license and regulate a wide range of
trades and professions, including barbers and cosmetologists, electricians and plumbers, and
architects and accountants, as well as the Office of Private Occupational School Education. DOL
also oversees the Office of Public Safety and Inspections, including an additional eleven public
safety boards, which do not contribute to the DOL Trust.
The 15 DOL boards of registration and examination that are subject to Section 35V establish and
administer the minimum qualifications for entering a licensed trade or profession, regulate the
practice of licensees, and take disciplinary action against licenses who violate licensing standards
and against non-licensees who practice without a license. Board members are not paid for their
service on the board and are generally members of the public appointed by the Governor. DOL
provides the centralized services and infrastructure necessary for the boards to function
effectively and efficiently. DOL employees include the executive directors of the boards, board
counsel, prosecuting attorneys, compliance officers, and hearings officers.
II. DOL TRUST BACKGROUND
The Legislature created the DOL Trust in May of 2002 to help the DOL overcome a substantial
backlog of disciplinary cases. Following statewide budget cuts in subsequent years and the repeal
2

of the DOL sunset provision, the DOL Trust became a permanent source of essential operating
funds for DOL, rather than a source of supplemental funding for enforcement.
Revenue for the Trust Fund comes primarily from the retention of licensing fees from the boards
of registration and examination, including 50% of licensing fee increases in and after Fiscal Year
1997 and 100% of license fee increases in or after Fiscal Year 2014. Therefore, the percentage
per board varies based upon the years that fees were increased.
III. DOL TRUST REVENUE AND EXPENDITURES
In Calendar Year 2025, January 1 – December 31, 2025, the DOL Trust collected $7,148,203 in
revenue, and expenditures totaled $7,036,380.00. These administrative costs are necessary to
carry out the DOL’s duties and functions required by state law.
Because DOL is structured to provide centralized services to the 15 boards and the Office of
Private Occupational School Education in the most cost-effective means possible, service
delivery is fully integrated. In most cases, any single staff member provides expertise in a
business function that serves multiple boards. All administrative, technical and enforcement
support services are delivered by centralized units. Executive directors, board counsel,
prosecuting attorneys, compliance officers, hearings officers, support staff, and managerial staff
work with multiple boards.
As a result, there is no per-board or per-unit chargeback system in place. All expenditures from
the trust fund are necessary to support this integrated operational model. These expenditures
include salaries/payroll, mailings, administrative costs, license printing and more. An itemized
listing of expenditures from 1/1/25 – 12/31/25 has been provided.

IV. CONCLUSION
While the DOL Trust was originally intended to be temporary, it has become essential to the
operation of the agency. Money in the DOL Trust funds portions of everything DOL does. This
includes rent and payroll, and it is critical to DOL’s continued operations.
DOL and the boards of registration and examination are central to the Commonwealth’s
economic development and public safety – licensing the people who do the jobs we depend on
and holding them accountable when they violate the law – and the agency uses every dollar it has
to meet the mission.
DOL respectfully requests the Legislature’s continued support as it performs this critical work.
