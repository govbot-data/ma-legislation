18 Section 38. (a) There is hereby established and set up on the books of the commonwealth
19 a separate fund, to be administered by the Executive Office of Housing and Livable
20 Communities and to be known as the Engaging Neighborhoods, Organizations, Unions,
21 Governments and Households Fund (the ENOUGH fund or the fund). The fund shall be credited
22 with: (i) appropriations, bond proceeds or other money authorized or transferred by the general
23 court and specifically designated to the fund; (ii) funds from public and private sources,
24 including, but not limited to, gifts, grants and donations; and (iii) any interest earned on such
25 money. Amounts credited to the fund shall not be subject to further appropriation and any money
26 remaining in the fund at the end of a fiscal year shall not revert to the General Fund and shall
27 remain available for purposes of the fund.
28 (b) Amounts credited to the fund shall be used to administer and fund the grant program
29 described in this section. Funds from this item may be used to support all partner organizations,
30 planning, operations and implementation, to include providing direct services to identified grant
31 program constituents. Funds may also be used to provide direct or third-party administrative
32 support to grantees, including technical assistance, capacity building and program evaluation.
33 Grantees shall receive support including, but not limited to, training from experts on best
34 practices in identifying inequities, prioritizing local needs, and making data-driven decisions. If
2 of 7

35 the fund is insufficient to fully fund the grant program, the Executive Office of Housing and
36 Livable Communities shall submit a request to the house and senate committees on ways and
37 means requesting additional funding to fully fund the program.
38 (c) The Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities, in consultation with the
39 ENOUGH fund advisory committee, shall develop a grant program to support community-
40 driven, place-based strategies aimed at reducing poverty. The general purpose of the fund shall
41 be to support collaborative efforts to improve outcomes and opportunities for children and
42 families living in the state’s most distressed neighborhoods and to transform those communities
43 by aligning housing and community development investments with access to a community-based
44 continuum of high-quality education, services, enrichment, and practices based on the best
45 available evidence that will address needs from birth through college and career. Specific
46 purposes of the grant program shall be to: (i) reduce concentrated poverty and expand economic
47 opportunity for people with low incomes; (ii) increase community health, well-being and safety;
48 (iii) provide cradle-to-career access to high-quality education, training and care; (iv) connect
49 residents to quality jobs and in-demand occupations; (v) enable family-sustaining income and
50 access to quality early education and care, affordable housing and health care, including
51 reproductive, maternal, behavioral and mental health care; (vi) provide high-quality support for
52 people with disabilities, families involved with the child welfare system, justice-involved youth
53 and adults and other vulnerable populations; (vii) connect individuals and families to appropriate
54 benefits and support programs; (viii) support students and children in attending and succeeding
55 in K-12 school environments through the expansion of integrated student support services and
56 enrichment programs; and (ix) leverage federal, local and private funding for communities and
57 neighborhoods. The grant shall ensure that a comprehensive approach to reducing poverty,
3 of 7

58 including childhood and intergenerational poverty, is applied across multiple sectors and systems
59 and is informed by data, evidence, research and best practices.
60 (d) Eligible entities must form place-based partnerships in geographically defined areas
61 to apply for the grant program. Eligible entities shall include non-profit organizations,
62 institutions of higher education, one or more public elementary, middle, or secondary schools,
63 community schools, school districts or offices of a school district superintendent, offices of a
64 chief elected official or city or town manager or units or agencies of local government, and
65 community-based organizations, to include neighborhood-based non-profit organizations. To be
66 eligible for funding, place-based partnerships must develop a comprehensive, effective
67 continuum of coordinated services in the following geographically defined areas: communities
68 where more than 20% of children live in poverty; communities with high concentrations of low-
69 income individuals; and/or communities with multiple signs of distress, which may include high
70 rates of concentrated poverty, childhood obesity, academic failure, chronic absenteeism, juvenile
71 delinquency, adjudication or incarceration.
72 (e) Grants shall be provided through the following tracks: (i) one-year partnership
73 development grants, which shall establish the foundation for proposed place-based strategy
74 through activities that include: convening partners, activating and engaging community leaders,
75 developing a formal partnership structure to conduct a shared vision for their community’s
76 success, and forming a shared accountability framework, to be used throughout the planning and
77 implementation phases; (ii) two-year plan development grants, which shall use the formal
78 partnership structure to conduct community asset mapping and needs assessment and engage in
79 participatory planning processes with community residents to develop comprehensive action
80 plans to increase economic mobility and significantly reduce poverty; and (iii) two-to-four-year
4 of 7

81 implementation grants, which shall launch approved action plans, leverage additional funding
82 streams, conduct progress monitoring and continuous quality improvement and adopt norms and
83 structures for ongoing community leadership and implementation of the action plan.
84 Implementation grants may be extended up to two years after the initial grant cycle provided
85 accountability data supports grant extension request.
86 (f) The Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities, in consultation with the
87 ENOUGH fund advisory committee, created pursuant to section 39 of this chapter, shall develop
88 a transparent and competitive process for the award of grants. The process shall include an
89 annual request for applications, widely disseminated through appropriate channels in at least the
90 top five spoken languages in the commonwealth. The Executive Office of Housing and Livable
91 Communities shall provide technical assistance and guidance for prospective applicants to ensure
92 equitable access to funding opportunities and shall, in consultation with the ENOUGH fund
93 advisory committee, develop a transparent and accountable process for reviewing applications.
94 Priority shall be given to applicants serving communities that have experienced long-term
95 economic distress, have demonstrated a commitment to cross-sector collaboration and
96 partnerships, and incorporate an equity lens as well as the engagement of people with lived
97 experience in their proposal.
98 Finalist applicants may be subject to site visits or interviews to assess readiness and
99 community involvement.
100 (g) Grantees shall be required to identify baseline and performance targets aligned with
101 metrics determined by the Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities, in
102 consultation with the ENOUGH fund advisory committee. Performance targets shall be aligned
5 of 7

103 with poverty reduction, economic security, improved education outcomes, increased health
104 outcomes and safe and thriving communities. Grantees may create research-practice partnerships
105 to improve their understanding of the cradle-to-career pipeline at the municipal or community
106 level. The ENOUGH fund advisory committee shall support these local efforts by coordinating
107 with relevant state agencies to link local data to data systems maintained by state agencies, in
108 compliance with all state and local federal laws to protect individual privacy.
109 (h) Annually, not later than October 1, the Executive Office of Housing and Livable
110 Communities shall submit a report to the governor, the clerks of the senate and house of
111 representatives and the senate and house committees on ways and means on the fund’s activities.
112 The report shall include: (i) the source and amount of funds received; (ii) the expenditures made
113 from the fund; (iii) the anticipated funding obligation for the next fiscal year; (iv) recipients of
114 grant funding; (v) size of awarded grants by recipient; (vi) summaries of funded projects; (vii)
115 performance data; (viii) challenges; and (ix) best practices. The report shall be posted on the
116 agency’s website.
117 Section 39. There shall be an engaging neighborhoods, organizations, unions,
118 governments and households (ENOUGH) fund advisory committee, the function of which shall
119 be advisory to the Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities in connection with the
120 management, operation and awarding of the fund. The committee shall consist of the following
121 members: the secretary of the Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities or her
122 designee; the secretary of the Executive Office of Health and Human Services or her designee;
123 the secretary of the Executive Office of Education or her designee; the secretary of the Executive
124 Office of Economic Development or her designee; the secretary of the Executive Office of Labor
125 and Workforce Development or her designee; the secretary of the Massachusetts Department of
6 of 7

126 Transportation or her designee; the commissioner of the Department of Transitional Assistance
127 or her designee; the president of the Massachusetts Development Finance Agency or her
128 designee; the director of rural affairs or her designee; 8 members to be appointed by the governor
129 who shall collectively have expertise as subject matter experts, providers, or people with lived
130 experience in a wide array of issues and areas that affect people in poverty, including but not
131 limited to cash benefits and other income supports, tax credits and tax assistance, asset
132 development and wealth building, early education and out-of-school time, K-12 education
133 including vocational education, higher education, workforce development and skills training,
134 labor unions and apprenticeships, immigration and refugee settlement, housing and
135 homelessness, nutrition and food security, public health, maternal health and infant support, early
136 intervention, transportation, environmental justice, health care, behavioral and mental health
137 care, trauma-sensitive intervention and violence prevention, social services, child welfare, digital
138 equity, justice-involved and re-entry services, legal services, and services for special populations
139 such as children and adults with disabilities, the LGBTQIA+ community, veterans, elders, and
140 youth aging out of foster care. All members shall be appointed for a term of 5 years, may be
141 reappointed, and shall serve without compensation, but may be reimbursed from the fund for
142 ordinary and reasonable in-state travel expenses. The committee may meet as often as the
143 members may determine, but shall meet at least bi-annually, or at such other intervals as may be
144 established by the agency in order to advise the agency, or such other qualified organization with
145 which the agency contracts, with respect to the fund and to make any advisory recommendations
146 with respect thereto to the agency. All grant applications recommended by the Executive Office
147 of Housing and Livable Communities shall be reviewed and approved by the advisory
148 committee.
