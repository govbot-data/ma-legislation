Background
Massachusetts employers submit over 30,000 injury reports to the Department of Industrial
Accidents (DIA) each year in Massachusetts. The submission of these reports indicates that the
employees have sustained injuries significant enough to lose 5 days or more of full or partial
wages. This staggering loss of income can be devastating, as these individuals are suddenly
deprived of the wages, they rely on to support themselves and their families. However, the true
cost of these workplace injuries extends far beyond just the financial burden - it is a deep
human toll that can have long-lasting, life-altering consequences. Many of those injured may
never be able to return to their previous jobs, forever altering their careers and livelihoods. In
the most tragic cases, some may never work again, their futures irrevocably changed by
preventable accidents.
What makes this situation even more distressing is the knowledge that so many of these
occupational injuries, illnesses, and even deaths can be avoidable. Proper education and
training for both workers and employers is critical in deterring, reducing, and potentially
eliminating these preventable tragedies.
History of the Workplace Safety Grant Program
The Workers' Compensation Act of 1985 enhanced the powers of the Department of Industrial
Accidents (DIA). Under the provisions of Massachusetts General Law, c. 23E, §3, the
Department of Industrial Accidents (DIA), Office of Safety is responsible for establishing
programs which include the education and training of employees and employers in the
recognition, avoidance, and prevention of unsafe or unhealthy work conditions.
In 1986, the DIA used this new authority to create the Office of Safety. The following year, in
1987, the Office of Safety issued its first Request for Response (RFR) for the fiscal grant year
1988. Ever since, the Office of Safety has managed the Occupational Safety Training and Health
Education Training Program, now called the Workplace Safety Grant Program.
The Workplace Safety Grant Program
The Workplace Safety Grant Program provides funding to help employers located and
operating within the Commonwealth prevent workplace injuries and promote safe, healthy
work environments. The program offers training, education, and other prevention initiatives for
employees and employer organizations in good standing and covered under Massachusetts
Workers' Compensation Law, (M.G.L Ch. 152).
The program has an annual budget of up to $800,000 and accepts individual or consortium
training proposals from employers. Applicants can receive a maximum award of $25,000 per
grant.
2

The Workplace Safety Grant Program provides that small, minority and women owned
businesses have the maximum practical opportunity to access funding. The language in our RFR
reflects an enhanced needs analysis, which helps determine if the organization has a financial
barrier, which may otherwise prohibit them from accessing quality-training services.
An Evaluation Committee appointed by the Director of the Department of Industrial Accidents
objectively evaluates all completed grant applications. Application assessments include training
goals, number of employees proposed to be trained, the cost effectiveness of proposed training
and a tangible timeline for training completion.
The Workplace Safety Grant Program requires that applicants redress an existing history of
injury or propose a training program designed to prevent future risk of injury. Following
applicant review, the Committee recommends a list of compelling applicants for funding, upon
approval of this list by the Director, Secretary of EOWLD and the Governor, funding is awarded.

Executive Summary:
The FY24 Workplace Safety Grant Program awarded $800,000 to 135 recipients, enabling 7,126
employees across 12 of the 14 eligible counties to receive workplace safety training at an
average cost of $112.26 per employee. Dukes and Nantucket counties were eligible counties
that did not have any applicants.
During the application period from October 2 to October 23, 2024, the Workplace Safety Grant
program received 163 applications which yielded $2.7 million in funding requests. However, six
applicants did not qualify for participation, nine funded recipients voluntarily withdrew due to
scheduling or staffing issues, and 13 recommended applicants could not be awarded grants
because all available funding had been exhausted.
The Grant Management System- GMS
The launch of the new Grant Management System in FY24 has significantly improved the
experience for both grant applicants and internal managers. Applicants can now apply for the
Workplace Safety Grant through a secure online portal, which automates and simplifies the
process, saving valuable time. The portal allows applicants to start their application, save their
work, and return to it later to gather additional information as needed. Once an application is
submitted, real-time communication and correspondence can occur between the applicant,
Office of Safety, the EOLWD Contracts & Procurement Unit, and Accounts Payable. For
successful grantees, the portal serves as the central hub for managing the grant. Awardees can
4

retrieve contract documents, upload class schedules, verify class completion, and request
payments - all within the portal. This integrated approach ensures data is seamlessly shared
among all stakeholders. Overall, the new Grant Management System has transformed the grant
lifecycle, delivering a more user-friendly and streamlined experience for both applicants and
grant managers.
FY24 Workplace Safety Grant Details
By collaborating with a diverse array of partners to include private companies, municipalities,
housing authorities, and non-profits like the Audubon Society and The Trustees of Reservations,
we were able to achieve several key outcomes. We partnered with Veterans, Inc. to train 175
employees across multiple locations. We also supported Signature Health/Brockton Hospital's
reopening efforts by training 252 nurses in violence mitigation and active shooter survival
strategies
Consortium grants allowed us to provide cost-effective training to a larger number of
employers. For instance, a grant to MassMEP, the Massachusetts Manufacturing Extension
Partnership, funded training for 156 employees across multiple small manufacturers. Another
grant supported the unique training program of the Franklin County Solid Waste Management,
which was delivered to employees in 21 towns. These programs were used to onboard new
hires as well as retrain existing staff.
In FY24, 20% of grant applicants requested training programs designed to mitigate workplace
violence. The funded training programs covered active shooter response, workplace de-
escalation techniques, and managing workplace violence. Future Works, an organization
seeking to keep social workers safe in adverse situations, employed trainers to provide both
lecture-based and hands-on training for active shooter survival.
Most trainings focused on fundamental safety topics, such as OSHA 10/30, electrical safety,
excavation, hoisting, rigging, hazardous waste handling, defensive driving, and first aid/CPR.
These trainings, which comprised nearly half of all granted requests, aimed to provide
employees with the knowledge and skills to prevent injury or death on the job.
With a focus on future growth, the DIA's Workplace Safety Grant Program hopes to expand its
budget and services to better support employers in the Commonwealth.
