MBTA’s Naloxone (Narcan)1 Pilot Distribution Program
In August 2024 the MBTA began a pilot program to make naloxone available to the public in select Red
Line stations. This program was funded via legislative appropriation, and naloxone was purchased through
partnership with the Department of Public Health. The MBTA installed distribution cabinets and made public
awareness messaging available in multiple languages. This report summarizes the MBTA’s activities and use of
the appropriated funds through the end of FY2025. Members of the public took naloxone from the cabinets,
but the MBTA is unaware of any instances of the provided naloxone having been used on site at the pilot
stations. The MBTA appreciates the challenges of substance abuse and overdose in the community and will
continue to ensure that all members of the Transit Police Department have naloxone and are properly trained
on its usage. However, given the challenges of temperature control for storage of naloxone and the staffing

Report and Accounting of MBTA Public-Access Naloxone Pilot
1. Which Red Line stations have accessible naloxone
Beginning July 31, 2024, the MBTA installed 15 cabinets containing naloxone for public access at five pilot
stations: Quincy Center, Ashmont, Andrew, South Station, and Harvard. At each station, cabinets were
installed in the unpaid and paid lobby areas as well as near or inside publicly accessible station restrooms. The
pilot stations were selected for high ridership, their proximity to available assistance from the Transit Police
Department in the event of an emergency, and because these stations offer a more temperature-controlled
environment which is required to store naloxone over extended periods of time. Signs in English, Spanish,
and Chinese were posted with each cabinet with information about how to recognize an opioid overdose,
how to administer naloxone, and how to contact emergency assistance.

4. Record Keeping, uptake and replacement rates

The MBTA replaced the naloxone as needed by station officials. In addition, the MBTA notes the following:
• Cabinets were checked daily and any missing naloxone was replaced; cabinets were consistently
stocked with naloxone.
• No cabinets have been reported damaged.
• There were no known instances of on-site use of naloxone from the cabinets or overdoses that can be
linked to the public-access naloxone. As far as the MBTA is aware, all doses taken from the cabinets
have been carried off site.
• The highest requests from MBTA staff for replacement inventory have been for Quincy Center, Andrew
and South Station.
5. Future of the program
During the pilot, the MBTA utilized existing red line operations personnel perform daily checks, record-keeping
and replacing naloxone in the cabinets. Inventory distribution and data collection added additional challenges
to MBTA operations staffing; the MBTA does not have an existing job classification suitable for long-term
implementation of a program of this nature across a subway line, and many Red Line stations are not
continuously staffed with dedicated personnel. During the pilot year, administrative staff at the MBTA’s Park
Plaza headquarters undertook the additional duty of ordering naloxone and personally delivering it to a
central location on the Red Line for distribution, but this procedure is not sustainable. Moreover, the MBTA
selected the five pilot stations based on the ability to store the naloxone in a relatively temperature-controlled
environment, and the logistics of installing electronic, temperature-controlled boxes and/or replacing
naloxone on a more frequent cadence during extreme heat or cold remain unknown.
The MBTA recognizes and supports the importance of promoting harm reduction efforts and increasing public
access to lifesaving medication that can reverse an opioid overdose. As a result of this pilot, the MBTA has
determined that it does not currently have sufficient resources or appropriate personnel needed to manage
the day-to-day administration of such a program. However, MBTA Transit Police Officers will continue to carry
naloxone.
The MBTA recommends that any public access program in or near stations be managed by a third party such
as an agency whose mission is aligned with such an important program and that has the resources and
personnel to monitor and resupply naloxone as well as to manage for temperature fluctuations. The MBTA
has conducted outreach to the Department of Public Health Bureau of Substance Addiction Services and to
Cambridge Health Alliance seeking assistance from these organizations, including suggestions for another
public health partner suitable to assume responsibility for the program. To date, no partner has been
identified. In the event staffing and program management can be made available from a third party, the
MBTA can continue to make space available in its facilities to support the program.
